THE THORPEDO ANNA EFFECT: WHY THE MODERN FILLY IS PUBLIC ENEMY NO. 1 FOR THE COL

Is the "fairer sex" of horse racing about to burn the clubhouse down? We aren’t just looking at a trend. We are witnessing a hostile takeover. For decades, the word "filly" suggested grace, a separate division, and a protected path to the Breeders’ Cup Distaff. That era is dead. Today, the word "filly"—specifically in the context of the 2024-2025 racing cycle—is a four-letter word to every trainer with a high-priced colt in his barn.

The sport of kings is being hijacked by queens. When Thorpedo Anna stepped onto the track at Saratoga this summer, the conversation shifted. It wasn't about whether she was the best three-year-old female in the country. That was obvious. The question became: is she the best athlete on four legs, period? When a filly starts out-running the boys’ speed figures, the industry panics. The betting windows shake. The "boys' club" starts looking for the nearest exit.

THE HARD TRUTH: THE COLTS ARE WEAK AND THE LADIES KNOW IT

Here is the reality that the bloodstock agents don’t want to admit: the current crop of three-year-old colts is erratic. We’ve seen a revolving door of winners in the Triple Crown races. No dominance. No clear leader. Into this vacuum stepped the fillies.

A "filly" is a female horse under the age of five. Usually, they stay in their lane. They run the Oaks. They run the Acorn. They stay away from the bruising physical reality of the classic distance against males. Not this year. The hard truth is that the genetic lottery has favored the females in recent crops. We are seeing fillies with higher bone density, better lung capacity, and, more importantly, a mental toughness that is lacking in their male counterparts.

The racing world is obsessed with "filly" right now because the talent gap has vanished. In the past, you’d need a freak of nature like Rachel Alexandra or Zenyatta to bridge that gap. Now? It feels like a systemic shift. The training regimens have evolved. The "soft" approach to fillies is gone. Trainers like Kenny McPeek are treating these girls like middleweights ready to hunt heavyweights.

TACTICAL BREAKDOWN: THE MECHANICS OF THE UPSET

How does a filly actually beat a colt? It’s not magic. It’s physics. Let’s look at the tactical profile of a top-tier filly like Thorpedo Anna or even the late-blooming stars hitting the entry gates this month.

First, look at the "turn of foot." In horse racing, the ability to accelerate while already at top speed is what separates a stakes winner from a claimer. High-end fillies often possess a leaner frame, allowing for a more efficient stride turnover. While the colts are lugging around massive muscle mass—which requires more oxygen and leads to faster fatigue—the elite filly is a model of aerobic efficiency.

In the Travers Stakes, we saw this play out in real-time. Thorpedo Anna didn't just "compete" against Fierceness. She stalked him. She put him in her crosshairs and didn't blink. She lost by a neck, but in doing so, she posted a Beyer Speed Figure that made every other colt in the division look like they were running in quicksand.

Tactically, fillies often benefit from being "the ghost in the machine." In a crowded field of colts, the males often get caught up in "testosterone battles"—fighting for the lead, bumping at the break, and trying to intimidate one another. An elite filly often sits just off the pace, ignored by the colts who don't view her as a threat until she’s blowing past them at the sixteenth pole. By then, it’s too late. The damage is done.

FAN REACTION: A DIVIDED RAIL

The "filly" phenomenon has split the fan base into two warring camps. On one side, you have the Traditionalists. These are the guys in the fedoras who believe the Kentucky Derby is for boys and the Kentucky Oaks is for girls. They argue that putting a filly in a race like the Breeders' Cup Classic is a waste of a career. They worry about "breaking" the horse. "Protect the asset," they scream.

Then you have the New Guard. The data-driven fans. The ones who look at the "sheets" and the Thoro-Graph numbers. They don't see gender; they see "figs." To them, if a filly runs a 110 Beyer and the best colt in the race runs a 105, you bet the filly. Period.

Social media has amplified this. Every time a filly wins a Grade 1, Twitter (X) explodes with calls for her to "run against the boys." The fans are tired of protected schedules. they want to see the best versus the best. They are tired of the "bubble-wrap" era of horse racing. When a filly like Thorpedo Anna takes on the males, the handle (the total amount bet) skyrockets. People want to see the giant-killer. They want to see the glass ceiling shattered on a dirt track.

HISTORICAL CONTEXT: THE GHOSTS OF GENUINE RISK AND RACHEL ALEXANDRA

To understand why the "filly" trend is so explosive right now, we have to look back. This isn't the first time the girls have raided the boys' locker room.

In 1980, Genuine Risk won the Kentucky Derby. She was only the second filly to do it. The world stopped. In 1988, Winning Colors did it again with a front-running performance that still gives bettors chills. But those were outliers.

The real turning point was 2009. Rachel Alexandra. She didn't just win; she humiliated the boys. She won the Preakness Stakes after starting from the outside post. She won the Haskell. She won the Woodward against older males. She was the "filly" that changed the definition of the word.

Then came Zenyatta. 19 wins in 20 starts. She won the Breeders' Cup Classic in 2009, coming from the back of the pack with a closing kick that looked like she had a turbocharger strapped to her girth. These horses proved that a filly isn't just a "female horse." An elite filly is a weapon of mass destruction. The current trend we see on Google and in the racing forums is a search for the next Rachel. The next Zenyatta.

PREDICTION: THE YEAR THE ECLIPSE AWARD TURNS PINK

Write this down. Put it in your bank account.

We are heading toward a historic collision at the Breeders' Cup. While the industry expects the fillies to stay in the Distaff, the data suggests they are the only ones capable of saving the Classic from being a snooze-fest.

I predict that within the next 12 months, we will see a filly not only win Horse of the Year but do so by winning at least two races against strictly male competition. The breeding industry is shifting. They are no longer just breeding for "early speed" to win a two-year-old sprint. They are breeding for the stamina that allows a female to stay 10 furlongs.

Furthermore, expect the betting markets to reflect this. The days of getting 10-1 odds on a filly in a "unisex" race are over. The sharps know. The algorithms know. If she’s in the gate, she’s the threat.

The three-year-old colt division is a mess. It’s a vacuum of talent. Thorpedo Anna and her sisters are going to fill that vacuum. We will see a filly win one of the Triple Crown races in 2025. It’s not a "maybe." It’s a mathematical certainty based on the current trajectory of speed figures.

FINAL VERDICT: STOP CALLING THEM "GIRLS"

The term "filly" is becoming a badge of intimidation. If you are an owner of a high-priced colt, seeing a top-tier filly enter your race is like seeing a shark in a swimming pool. You can complain about the "unfairness" of the weight break (fillies usually carry a few pounds less than colts), but weight isn't what’s winning these races.

Heart is.

The modern filly is tougher, more consistent, and currently faster than the boys. The sport needs this. Horse racing has been looking for a superstar to bridge the gap between the casual fan and the hardcore bettor. We found her. She doesn't have a stallion contract waiting for her. She doesn't have a future as a "stud." She has one job: to run.

The "filly" isn't just a category anymore. She is the headline. If you’re still betting against them because of an old-school bias, you aren't just a dinosaur. You're a broke dinosaur. The girls are running the show now. Get used to it.
